What is Digital Accessibility?

Digital accessibility is more than just website accessibility. Digital accessibility also includes any content that is put on the internet or any content that is available digitally. Content like educational materials stored on an LMS such as Canvas or Blackboard or even Google Classroom needs to be accessible so that students are able to properly be able to learn from and iteract with the content without limitations due to accessibility related topics. Below are some recommendations on how to make both Websites and Documents Accessible:

Use Proper Heading Structure

Screen Reader programs allow for individuals to independantly navigate not only technology but the world wide web. Screen Reader users have the ability to navigate utilizing keyboard commands that allow for them to search for specific things like level 2 headers, hyperlinks, tables, and much more. When these items aren't properly configured or coded correctly then the individual won't be able to access the materials.

Alternative Text for Images, Graphs, and Tables

Imagine this... you are unable to see what is on your computer screen due to losing your vision. Your screen reader keeps reading "image, image, image, image, graphic" over and over. You get frustrated and move on to the next thing without realizing that the content on those images would have important information on how to complete your homework for the week. This is the reality for many students as screen reader software can't "read images", they can only "read" the Alternative Text that is coded for the image. Without that Alternative Text many images will read as either graphic or image and some won't read at all, which is one big reason why images are not recommended to be the primeray source of information when creating content. The catch with Alternative Text is that it needs to be descriptive enough without overloading the user with information and also needs to be under two sentences in length.
